title: "\U0001F527 The Spreadsheet Was Never the Job"
pubDate: '2026-07-14T21:06:03-04:00'
description: >-
  On OpenAI's ChatGPT Work launch, and what actually happens to a job once the
  recurring tasks stop needing a person to trigger them.
---
OpenAI's newest release is an agent built into ChatGPT that stays open for hours and comes back with a finished spreadsheet, a formatted document, a working dashboard, rolled out this week under the banner of a tool for your entire workday. The demo footage is the demo footage every one of these launches uses: type a request, walk away, come back to something that used to take real effort to produce by hand. I've watched a version of that demo before, except mine never made it into a keynote. It was a flow that read an Excel sheet and created recurring tasks on a schedule, and a report that used to be a spreadsheet someone rebuilt from scratch every month.

The pitch this time is that the agent doesn't just draft, it ships, finished output, not a rough pass a person cleans up afterward. That's a real distinction and I don't want to wave it away. If an agent can reliably hand back a dashboard that's actually correct without someone re-checking every join and every filter, that changes which junior tasks exist at all, not just how long they take. The strongest version of the skeptical case against what I'm about to argue is genuinely there: some of what analysts and coordinators do is structured enough that an agent doing it well means fewer people are needed to do it. Pretending otherwise is its own kind of denial, and I've seen enough of this category of tool work well to know the skeptics aren't just being precious about their job titles.

But *reliably correct* is carrying almost the entire weight of that sentence, and it's the part the coverage of this launch keeps skipping past. I build automations for a research team at a large hospital network, and a good chunk of what I do eats the exact category of task this new agent is being sold on: recurring document generation, scheduled data pulls, dashboards that used to be assembled by hand every cycle. None of that made my job smaller. It moved the job somewhere else. The flow that creates the tasks doesn't know which task actually matters this cycle. The dashboard that refreshes the numbers doesn't know that a lookup column somewhere upstream just picked up a duplicate value, quietly making every relationship built on top of it wrong. Someone still has to catch that, and catch it before a decision gets made on the bad number. The job didn't shrink so much as move upstream, into judging what the automation produced instead of producing it by hand.

That's the part most coverage of a launch like this glosses over, because "the agent now ships instead of drafts" is a cleaner story than "someone still has to decide whether to trust what got shipped." A structured task done wrong at scale is just a structured task done wrong faster, with more confidence behind the wrong answer than a human draft would have carried. The organizations that get real value out of a tool like this generally aren't the ones that pointed an agent at a workflow and walked away. They're the ones that kept a person accountable for catching the plausible-looking wrong answer, because anywhere a bad number costs more than a redo, that check doesn't go away just because the draft got better.

What actually happened to my own hours, once the recurring tasks stopped needing a person to trigger them by hand, is that the time went somewhere I didn't expect: figuring out why a report had been showing the wrong number for weeks before anyone noticed, deciding what a flow should do when the input data doesn't look like what it was built to expect, explaining to people who don't build these things why the automation isn't magic and still needs a person who understands what it's touching. None of that shows up in a productivity report as hours saved, because it isn't the thing being measured. It's the thing that has to exist for the measured part to be trustworthy at all.

I don't doubt the new agent will save people real hours this year, the way mine did for me. What I doubt is that the hours saved is the whole story, or even the interesting part of it. The interesting part is what the person whose hours got saved is doing with them now, six months into using a tool like this, once the novelty of the finished spreadsheet wears off and someone has to answer for what's actually in it.

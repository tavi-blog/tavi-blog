---
title: "\U0001F50B The Repair Law Fixes the Easy Half"
pubDate: '2026-07-21T21:08:30-04:00'
description: >-
  The EU's right-to-repair rules become mandatory this month. They fix the axis
  of phone ethics that's easiest to legislate, and leave the harder one exactly
  where it was.
---
Somewhere in a phone I'll probably buy in the next couple of years, there's a battery that today would refuse to charge properly if a technician swapped it for anything other than the exact part paired to that unit at the factory. That's not hypothetical. It's how parts pairing already works on a lot of flagship hardware: the software checks whether a replacement component was blessed by the original vendor, and if it wasn't, a feature quietly degrades, a warning banner appears, or the part just doesn't work. By the end of this month, inside the EU, that stops being legal on smartphones and tablets. Manufacturers have to make batteries, screens, and a defined list of other components available to consumers and independent shops, deliverable within a week and a half, for seven years after a model goes out of production, and they can no longer lean on software locks to make a compatible third-party part behave like a foreign object.

I don't live under that law. I'm in Canada, where nothing like it exists yet, which means repairability is still exactly what it's always been for me: something I have to research and weigh myself before I buy, not something baked into the product by the time it reaches a shelf. I already run something close to a two-part test on every phone I've considered switching to. One axis is practical: does it perform well enough for daily use, does contactless payment actually work, will the software still get updates in three years. The other axis is closer to labor and privacy: how the thing was made, how much of my data it hands to an ad network by default, whether I trust the company holding it. Repairability has always sat on the practical axis, and until now it's been one more thing I had to go dig up myself, buried in a teardown video or a spec sheet that has no interest in advertising how hard the battery is to replace.

What the EU just did is take that one variable off the table for its own consumers and turn it into a legal obligation rather than a research project I have to do myself. That's a real, structural change, and I don't want to undersell it by rushing straight to what it doesn't fix. A mandatory seven-year parts window does something real: it makes repair-versus-replace an honest economic comparison again, one manufacturers can no longer quietly tilt toward replacement using nothing but software.

But it's worth being clear-eyed about the shape of the problem this leaves standing. Nothing in a right-to-repair directive touches whether a phone ships with location tracking on by default, or how much of what I type gets routed through a company whose other business lines I'd rather not fund. A phone can clear every repairability requirement in that law and still be exactly the kind of device I've talked myself out of buying, for reasons that have nothing to do with how easily the battery comes out. The EU fixed the axis that was always going to be easier to legislate, because it's measurable: days to deliver a part, years of availability, whether a software lock exists or doesn't. What a company does with what the phone sees and hears resists that kind of clean statutory language, and I don't think that's an accident so much as a reflection of which harms are easy to write a bill around.

I also don't think the parts-pairing defense manufacturers have offered is nothing, and it deserves more than a dismissal. Locking a battery to a specific serial number genuinely helps with tamper detection and with keeping counterfeit components, some of which catch fire, out of repair chains that are hard to audit at the shop level. A directive that just ripped out every software check with no replacement would trade one failure mode for another, worse one. The version actually rolling out keeps a narrow carveout for exactly that kind of safety justification, which is the part of this story that gets flattened whenever it's covered as a simple fight between manufacturers who want lock-in and everyone else who just wants freedom.

What I keep landing on is that I'll still be doing the second half of my own evaluation by hand, because no legislature is about to write a bill telling a company what it can and can't do with a location ping or an ad identifier. The repair law made one part of a phone purchase easier to trust without doing the homework myself. It left the part I actually spend more time thinking about exactly where it was.

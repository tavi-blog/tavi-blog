---
title: "\U0001F916 Deployed, Not Trusted Yet"
pubDate: '2026-07-11T20:41:58-04:00'
---
A healthcare technology forecast making the rounds this year predicts that by December, most hospitals and health systems will have at least one AI agent running somewhere in a clinical or administrative workflow. Reading through it, the number itself didn't stick with me. The word ahead of it did. Deployed. It's a tidy word for something that, from where I sit, is nowhere near tidy.

The forecast isn't wrong to be optimistic, and it's worth taking the optimism seriously before picking at it. Fewer claim denials. Faster prior authorizations. Physicians getting back hours a week they currently lose typing into systems that were never designed around their day. These aren't invented numbers. Ambient documentation tools are seeing genuine adoption, and the executives reporting returns on them aren't exaggerating. If the story stopped at the top-line statistics, it would be a simple one: healthcare found a tool it needed and started using it.

The reports lean on real pain points, too. Physicians are estimated to spend upward of sixteen hours a week on documentation, prior authorizations, and billing, and burnout tied to administrative load is running past half the workforce by some counts. Against numbers like that, an agent that can draft a note or chase a prior authorization without a human typing every field looks less like a novelty and more like relief that's overdue. It's worth sitting with that for a second before moving on, because the counterargument to what follows has to survive contact with real hours given back to real people, not just a vendor's slide.

But deployed and trusted are two different states, and the gap between them is where I actually live. I help build systems like this inside a large hospital network, and getting one running was never the hard part. The hard part is that an institution like this can't afford a confidently wrong answer the way a smaller, faster-moving company can. A bad recommendation from a consumer app costs someone an annoying afternoon. A wrong answer about a regulatory submission deadline can cost a research team its funding window entirely. So the systems that actually make it into use tend to be narrower than the underlying technology allows, on purpose. Speed gets traded for scope, every time, because the cost of being wrong once outweighs the benefit of being fast a hundred times.

That tradeoff is structural, not anecdotal, and it's the part a survey of executives is never going to capture, because executives sit at the furthest possible distance from the moment an answer turns out to be wrong. The distance gets absorbed somewhere further down the chain, by whoever actually has to check the machine's answer against the real regulation before acting on it. That's not a flaw anyone is hiding from the forecast. It's just not the kind of thing that shows up as a line item in a report about return on investment.

None of this cancels out the genuine improvement underneath it. An agent that can answer a routine compliance question correctly at midnight, when no one else is awake to answer it, beats a two-day wait in a shared inbox by any reasonable measure. That's real, and it matters to the person who was stuck waiting. The people building and selling these tools aren't wrong about what the technology can do. What the forecast leaves out is the institution the technology has to operate inside, which carries its own decades-old logic about what happens when a system tells the wrong person the wrong thing at the wrong moment, logic that doesn't show up anywhere in an adoption percentage.

The tools get built by teams that can ship fast and patch just as fast. Hospitals run on a different clock, one where a mistake doesn't get patched quietly, it gets audited. That mismatch is the actual translation work happening underneath the adoption numbers, and it's slower and far less quotable than a forecast headline. It looks like narrowing what a given agent is allowed to know until the risk is acceptable, and accepting that the more cautious version is usually the responsible one, even when it's also the less impressive one to present.

This isn't a healthcare-specific problem, either, it's just easiest to see there because the stakes are so immediate. Any adoption number, in any regulated field, measures how many organizations flipped a switch. It doesn't measure how many of the people downstream of that switch still quietly redo the work by hand, because the institution hasn't decided, collectively, that it's safe not to. Coverage of new technology tends to treat the switch-flipping as the finish line, because a percentage is a clean thing to report and a slow, uneven accumulation of trust is not. The gap between the two isn't a failure of the technology. It's just a slower story than the one the forecast is telling.

So I believe the forecast and I distrust what it implies in the same breath. Most health systems will have an agent live somewhere in their workflow by the end of the year. That tells you almost nothing about how much of the institution actually relies on what it says without checking, or how much quiet human verification is sitting behind every fast answer, absorbing risk that never makes it into the report. The deployment is real. The trust is still being built by hand, case by case, in a place no survey is set up to measure.
